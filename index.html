Breathing
Malaysia

A data-driven investigation into Malaysia's air pollution patterns from 2017 to 2022 — who is exposed, what pollutants dominate, and when the air turns dangerous.

01

Where do the most people breathe this air?

State-level choropleth showing 2025 population. The air pollution dataset is national monthly data, so this contextualises exposure risk — not the pollution level itself. Selangor and KL concentrations represent Malaysia's largest exposure zone.

02

Population density reveals unequal exposure

Bubble size encodes absolute 2025 population. Large geographic states like Sarawak and Sabah host far fewer people than Peninsular urban corridors — geography alone misrepresents risk.

03

PM2.5 — how often does Malaysia exceed the WHO limit?

The WHO annual guideline for PM2.5 is 15 µg/m³. Area above the threshold is shaded in amber — a technique that makes exceedances immediately visible without requiring the viewer to mentally calculate.

⚠ WHO annual guideline: 15 µg/m³
04

All pollutants together — a streamgraph of indexed change

Each band represents one pollutant's indexed concentration (first month = 100). The 2019 haze event is visible as a dramatic co-spike in PM2.5, PM10, and CO, while O3 and NO2 follow different cycles.

05

Pollutant rankings shift over time

A bump chart tracks each pollutant's annual rank by mean concentration. Rank inversions — where one pollutant overtakes another year-on-year — reveal policy effects, seasonal patterns, and data anomalies better than trend lines alone.

06

The PM2.5 calendar — spotting repeat offenders

Each cell is one month. The heatmap surface makes seasonal patterns legible at a glance: mid-year months (Jun–Sep) show elevated PM2.5 levels across multiple years, consistent with the Southwest monsoon haze period.

07

The pollution clock — PM2.5 by month of year

Bars radiate from the centre like clock hands — longer bars indicate higher average PM2.5. The visual immediately shows which seasons are worst without needing to read axis labels.

08

Top 10 worst months — with WHO reference

Use the dropdown to explore any pollutant. A lollipop chart reduces ink-to-data ratio versus a plain bar chart, while the WHO guideline line (where applicable) makes exceedances immediately apparent.

09

PM2.5 vs PM10 — do they rise and fall together?

A regression line and confidence band overlaid on the scatter. The strong positive correlation confirms a shared source (combustion, haze events). Outliers — months where the two diverge — are labelled automatically.

11

Pollutant co-occurrence — a chord diagram

Each arc represents a pollutant. Ribbons connect pairs that tend to spike together in the same month — thicker ribbons mean stronger co-occurrence. PM2.5 and PM10 share the widest ribbon, confirming a common combustion source. Hover over an arc to isolate that pollutant's connections.

12

From year to pollutant to risk level — a Sankey flow

Flow width encodes the number of monthly readings at each risk level (Low <50% of max, Medium 50–80%, High >80%). The diagram reveals which years contributed most high-risk months, and for which pollutants.

13

State population at a glance — a bubble cloud

Each bubble represents a Malaysian state. Larger, brighter bubbles mean more people — the population figure is labelled inside. The layout packs states by size using force simulation, making the Selangor / KL dominance immediately apparent without needing to read a map.

14

Pollutant fingerprint — Malaysia's air quality profile

The radial spider chart normalises each pollutant by its own peak, so all six can be compared on the same canvas. A longer spoke means the pollutant's typical level is closer to its worst-ever reading — indicating persistent rather than sporadic exposure.
